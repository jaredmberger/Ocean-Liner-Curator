MS Bermuda

Furness Bermuda Line · 1928 · Ship Guide

Overview

MS Bermuda was a distinctive interwar diesel passenger liner built for the New York–Bermuda route, linking the American east coast with Hamilton in a service world shaped by tourism rather than transatlantic migration or express-liner competition. She is especially significant as one of the early large motor liners in passenger service and as a ship whose identity was tied closely to a fashionable destination trade. Her career, however, was relatively brief and was marked by repeated fire damage before ending in total loss.

In collecting and interpretation, Bermuda is best divided into three main phases: original New York–Hamilton passenger service, post-fire rebuilt service, and final loss. Material from those phases should be distinguished carefully, since the ship’s interiors and service presentation changed after the 1931 fires.

Design & Construction Context

Bermuda was not designed for the classic North Atlantic immigrant or express trade. Instead, she belonged to the destination-liner world of leisure travel between New York and Bermuda, where passenger comfort, novelty, and resort appeal mattered more than record-breaking crossings. That context is central to interpreting the ship properly.

She was also notable as a large motor ship at a time when diesel propulsion in passenger service still carried a sense of modernity and technical distinction. In curatorial terms, this makes her relevant not only as a Bermuda route vessel but also as an example of propulsion change in interwar passenger shipping.

Service History (Summary)

1927–1928: Built by Workman, Clark & Co. at Belfast, Bermuda was launched in July 1927, completed late that year, and entered service in January 1928. She was built specifically for the New York–Hamilton, Bermuda route.

Early route identity: In peacetime passenger service she operated as a luxury destination liner carrying an all-first-class complement to Bermuda. This is the correct interpretive setting for brochures, menus, passenger lists, luggage labels, and commercial imagery tied to her original civilian identity.

Diesel-liner significance: Her motor-ship machinery gave her a modern profile in the late 1920s and helped distinguish her from more conventional steam-driven passenger ships. This technical identity is part of what makes her historically interesting.

1931 fires: In 1931 the ship suffered two major fires, the second causing especially severe damage. These casualty events divide the ship’s history into materially different interpretive phases, since post-fire service references may not correspond neatly to her as-built interior world.

After the fires: Although there was an attempt to preserve value through disposal and towing rather than immediate normal continuation of service, the ship’s commercial future had effectively been broken by the repeated fire damage.

1933 loss: While under tow after sale for further disposal, Bermuda was wrecked and became a total loss. Her career therefore ended not in routine scrapping after long service, but in a casualty sequence following severe onboard fire damage.

Interpretive Notes

This is a Bermuda destination liner first: Bermuda should be understood primarily through the New York–Hamilton leisure-travel context, not through North Atlantic liner-race assumptions.

The all-first-class profile matters: this was part of the ship’s commercial identity and helps explain the tone of surviving publicity and passenger material.

Diesel propulsion is central to the ship’s significance: Bermuda belongs to the early wave of large passenger motor ships and therefore fits into the broader technological story of interwar liner design.

Pre-fire and post-fire material should not be collapsed together: the 1931 casualty events likely altered both the physical ship and the interpretive meaning of later references.

Cataloging note on prefix: many references style the ship as MS Bermuda, and that is generally the more accurate form for a motor ship.
