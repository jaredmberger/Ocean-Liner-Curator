SS Majestic

White Star Line · 1889 · Ship Guide

Overview

SS (often styled RMS) Majestic (1889) was one of White Star Line’s late-Victorian flagship liners, built by Harland & Wolff as the sister ship to Teutonic. Entering service in 1890, she represents White Star’s pivot into the high-speed, twin-screw era—steel construction, powerful triple-expansion machinery, and a public identity calibrated to compete for prestige on the North Atlantic.

Her most collectible “headline” is time-specific: in July 1891 Majestic secured the westbound transatlantic speed record (a Blue Riband-era record claim), held briefly before being eclipsed. As with all speed-record collecting, the direction, measurement basis, and exact dates matter.

Evidence-first note: “RMS” appears widely in modern references, but artifacts and period print can be inconsistent with prefixes. Catalog what the object prints (line, route, date, ports) first; add prefix language only when supported.

Design & Construction (Context)

Majestic (and her sister Teutonic) is a useful “hinge ship” for understanding White Star’s evolution: the leap from the earlier 1870s generation into a new class of fast, steel-hulled liners designed to compete directly in the Atlantic prestige race. She is commonly cited as among White Star’s flagships of the 1890s—large, fast, and public-facing.

Cataloging tip: late-Victorian White Star ephemera often highlights “new liners,” speed, and modernity. If a piece claims “Blue Riband” or “fastest crossing,” pin it to a dated voyage (direction + month/year), then verify the claim against compiled record tables or contemporary reporting.

Service History (Summary)

1890: entry into service. After completion in March 1890, Majestic departed Liverpool on 2 April 1890 for New York on her maiden voyage, joining White Star’s premier Atlantic service.

1891: speed record moment. In July 1891, she achieved a celebrated westbound record crossing—often summarized in Blue Riband lists as 5 days 18 hours 8 minutes with an average around 20.1 knots—a distinction held only briefly.

1890s–early 1910s: long working life. Across a 24-year career she remained in transatlantic service as newer, larger ships entered the fleet. Late-career route descriptions commonly include Liverpool/Queenstown/New York earlier, with later Southampton/Cherbourg/New York service in the 1900s.

1914: breaking. With obsolescence and changing economics, she was sold for scrapping; breaking is documented as commencing on 5 May 1914 at Morecambe (Thos. W. Ward).

Interpretive Notes

Collecting Majestic is often about anchoring a piece to one of two identities: (1) “flagship White Star express liner” (1890s prestige), or (2) “the record voyage moment” (July 1891 westbound). Both require date discipline—especially because “Majestic” is a reused name across maritime history.

Practical checks:
1) Route anchor: Liverpool/Queenstown/New York vs. Southampton/Cherbourg/New York helps place era.
2) Name confusion: Always tag “(1889)” in your cataloging; do not rely on “RMS” alone.
3) Record language: Treat “Blue Riband” as a claim that must specify direction and date; avoid blanket wording.
4) Build identifiers: Harland & Wolff Yard No. 209 is a strong cross-check for serious listings and research notes.
