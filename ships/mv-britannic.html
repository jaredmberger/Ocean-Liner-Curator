MV Britannic

White Star Line · 1930 · Ship Guide

Overview

MV Britannic was a diesel-powered “cabin ship” built by Harland & Wolff for the White Star Line and placed in service in 1930. Along with her near-sister MV Georgic (1932), she represented White Star’s interwar shift toward economical operation and modern passenger comfort.

After White Star’s 1934 merger, she served under Cunard-White Star and later Cunard. Requisitioned in World War II, she survived and returned to postwar transatlantic service, ultimately becoming the last White Star–built liner in commercial operation.

Name-confusion note: “Britannic” spans multiple White Star ships—RMS Britannic (1874), HMHS Britannic (launched 1914; lost 1916), and MV Britannic (this ship; launched 1929; in service from 1930). Always document which era your object belongs to.

Design & Construction Context

MV Britannic was built as an interwar “cabin ship,” prioritizing paying-class comfort and operating economy rather than record speed. Diesel propulsion reduced fuel-handling demands and supported consistent, economical schedules—an important strategic choice during the cost pressures of the 1930s.

Service History (Summary)

Entering service in 1930, Britannic ran transatlantic service and also undertook cruising from early in her career. After the 1934 merger, she continued under Cunard-White Star branding and later Cunard.

Requisitioned for wartime service, she carried troops and survived the conflict. A major postwar overhaul preceded her return to the Atlantic, where she remained in service into 1960.

Interpretive Notes

Collecting attribution benefits from “phase labeling.” MV Britannic materials often carry late White Star / Cunard-White Star branding, interwar typography, and route naming that differs sharply from pre-1914 White Star design language. When possible, document: printer/stationer, address blocks, route panels (ports called), and any dated event context.
